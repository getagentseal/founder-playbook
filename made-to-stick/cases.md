# Cases - Made to Stick

## Organized by SUCCESs Principle

---

## SIMPLE

### Southwest Airlines - "THE Low-Fare Airline"

**The core:** Herb Kelleher reduced Southwest's entire strategy to five words: "THE low-fare airline."

**How it works as Commander's Intent:** When the marketing department proposed adding chicken Caesar salad to flights (research showed passengers wanted it), Kelleher asked: "Will adding chicken Caesar salad help us become THE low-fare airline?" No. No salad. Decision made in seconds.

**Why it's sticky:** Every employee, from gate agents to executives, can resolve any ambiguity using this filter. It generates correct decisions across thousands of situations without a policy manual.

**Principle demonstrated:** Forced prioritization. "If you say three things, you say nothing."

---

### Clinton 1992 - "It's the Economy, Stupid"

**The core:** James Carville wrote three phrases on a whiteboard in campaign headquarters:
1. "Change vs. more of the same"
2. "The economy, stupid"
3. "Don't forget health care"

**How it worked:** "It's the economy, stupid" became the Commander's Intent for thousands of campaign workers making independent decisions daily. Every speech, every ad, every talking point could be tested against it.

**Principle demonstrated:** Commander's Intent scales. One clear core message can align an army of people who never directly coordinate with each other.

---

### Palm Pilot - The Wooden Block

**The core:** Jeff Hawkins carved a wooden block the size of a shirt pocket and carried it for months as a "mockup" of the future Palm Pilot. He'd pull it out in meetings, pretend to tap on it, check schedules.

**How it worked:** The wooden block was a physical Commander's Intent. Every proposed feature had to fit. When engineers wanted to add capabilities, Hawkins pulled out the block: "Does it still fit in a pocket?" The constraint drove simplicity.

**Principle demonstrated:** Concrete simplicity constraints beat abstract mission statements.

## UNEXPECTED

### Nordstrom Tire Refund

**The story:** A man brought a set of tires to Nordstrom for a refund. Nordstrom doesn't sell tires. The sales associate refunded him anyway.

**Why it's sticky:** It violates our schema ("stores only refund products they sell") but is postdictable - once you know Nordstrom's legendary customer service policy, the refund makes sense. The surprise reinforces rather than contradicts the core message.

**How it spread:** This story has been told and retold by Nordstrom employees and customers for decades. It communicates more about Nordstrom's service culture than any training manual could.

**Principle demonstrated:** Surprise must be postdictable. Schema violation that reinforces the core message.

---

### Nora Ephron - "No School Thursday"

**The story:** Nora Ephron's journalism teacher gave students a set of facts: the school principal announced several schedule changes, and Thursday would have no school. Students wrote standard leads about the principal's announcement. The teacher's response: "The lead is: 'There will be no school Thursday.'"

**Why it matters:** The unexpected angle is what the AUDIENCE cares about, not the procedural facts. Students were trained to find the surprising human angle, not to dutifully record events.

**Principle demonstrated:** Finding the unexpected requires empathizing with the audience. What surprises THEM, not what surprises you.

---

### Roone Arledge - Monday Night Football

**The problem:** Sports broadcasting before Arledge was static cameras pointed at the field. Boring for non-fans.

**The fix:** Arledge asked: "Why would someone who doesn't care about football watch this?" He introduced sideline cameras, personal stories about players, personality in the broadcast booth, unexpected camera angles.

**Principle demonstrated:** The question "Why would a NON-fan care?" forces you to find the unexpected hook in any subject.

## CONCRETE

### Jane Elliott - Brown Eyes / Blue Eyes (1968)

**Setup:** Third-grade teacher in Riceville, Iowa. Day after Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination.

**The exercise:** Divided students by eye color. Brown-eyed children were "superior" for one day - extra recess, sat in front, went first. Blue-eyed children wore collars, were criticized, restricted. Next day: reversed.

**Results:** Academic performance shifted with status assignment. Phonics card pack: 5.5 minutes when "inferior," 2.5 minutes when "superior." Students described themselves as "sad, bad, stupid, and mean" when in the inferior group.

**Long-term impact:** Studies 10 and 20 years later showed Elliott's students were significantly less prejudiced than peers. Former students still describe it as the most powerful learning experience of their lives.

**Principle demonstrated:** Concrete experience of an abstract concept (discrimination) creates hooks so deep they last decades. Elliott turned prejudice from a word into a feeling.

### Boeing 727 - "Land on Runway 4-22"

**The abstract goal:** "Build the best passenger plane in the world."

**The concrete goal:** "Seat 131 passengers, fly nonstop from Miami to New York City, and land on Runway 4-22 at La Guardia." Runway 4-22 was less than a mile - too short for existing jets.

**Why it worked:** Thousands of engineers and manufacturing workers could coordinate because there was zero ambiguity. Every design decision could be tested: "Does it still seat 131? Does it land in under a mile?"

**Principle demonstrated:** Concrete goals enable coordination at scale. Abstract goals produce parallel but misaligned efforts.

### Hamburger Helper "Fingertips" Program

**Setup:** Hamburger Helper had been in a decade-long slump. Brand manager Melissa Studzinski inherited three "death binders" of abstract data.

**The change:** Sent marketing, advertising, and R&D staff into actual customer homes. Called it "Fingertips" - putting the customer at your fingertips.

**Key insight:** Moms and kids valued predictability, not variety. Kids didn't care about 11 pasta shapes. They cared about finding the same flavor they liked. With 30+ flavors, moms couldn't find favorites on shelves.

**Result:** Simplified the product line. Sales increased 11% in one fiscal year after a decade of decline.

**Studzinski's quote:** "Now when I've got a decision to make about the brand, I think of the women I met."

**Principle demonstrated:** Abstract data ("death binders") produces abstract strategy. Concrete customer observation produces actionable insight.

### Kaplan's Maroon Portfolio

**Setup:** Jerry Kaplan pitched Kleiner Perkins VCs with no slides, no business plan, no prototype. Just a maroon leather portfolio.

**The moment:** Mid-pitch, Kaplan said: "If I were carrying a portable PC right now, you would sure as hell know it. You probably didn't realize that I am holding a model of the future of computing right here in my hands." He tossed the portfolio onto the table.

**What happened:** A stunned silence. One partner slowly reached out to touch it "as if it were some sort of talisman." Then the VCs started brainstorming excitedly among themselves. Kaplan barely had to speak.

**Result:** Kleiner Perkins backed the idea at a $4.5M valuation for a nonexistent company.

**Principle demonstrated:** A concrete prop transforms a defensive grilling session into a collaborative brainstorm. The portfolio invited the VCs to bring THEIR expertise to bear.

### James Grant - Salt and Sugar Packet (UNICEF)

**Setup:** Millions of children dying from dehydration. Complex medical problem with a simple solution (oral rehydration salts).

**The moment:** When meeting prime ministers, Grant always carried a small packet of salt and sugar. He'd hold it up: "Do you know that this costs less than a cup of tea and it can save hundreds of thousands of children's lives in your country?"

**Principle demonstrated:** A concrete prop + an unexpected comparison (less than tea / saves thousands) can motivate heads of state. The packet, like Kaplan's portfolio, invites the audience to bring their own expertise.

## CREDIBLE

### Pam Laffin - The Anti-Authority

**Setup:** Anti-smoking campaign needed a spokesperson.

**The choice:** Not a doctor. Not a celebrity. Pam Laffin - a 29-year-old mother of two who started smoking at age 10, developed emphysema by 24, suffered a failed lung transplant.

**The campaign:** Filmed brutal 30-second spots showing Laffin battling to breathe, enduring invasive bronchoscopy, displaying surgical scars. She said: "I started smoking to look older and I'm sorry to say it worked."

**Impact:** Became a heroine of the anti-smoking movement. Subject of MTV documentary, CDC campaign, educational video. She died at age 31, three weeks before her second transplant.

**Principle demonstrated:** Anti-authorities (people with lived experience and nothing to gain) are often more credible than experts or celebrities. Honesty > status.

### Beyond War - 5,000 BBs

**Setup:** 1980s citizen group alarmed by the nuclear arms race. Arranged "house parties" to communicate the scale of the threat.

**The demonstration:**
1. Drop one BB into a metal bucket. CLANG. "This is the Hiroshima bomb."
2. Describe Hiroshima's devastation.
3. Drop 10 BBs. Louder clatter. "This is one nuclear submarine."
4. Ask attendees to close their eyes.
5. Pour 5,000 BBs. "This is the world's current arsenal."
6. The roar goes on and on. Dead silence after.

**Principle demonstrated:** Statistics illustrate relationships, not standalone numbers. The relationship (1 → 10 → 5,000) is what stuck. Nobody remembered the exact number. Everyone remembered the roar.

### Safexpress - Harry Potter and the Sinatra Test

**Setup:** Indian logistics company needed to win a Bollywood studio's film distribution deal. Studio was skeptical.

**The credential:** Safexpress had delivered every Harry Potter book to every bookstore in India, arriving at exactly 8 AM on release day. Not too early (prevent leaks), not too late (prevent lost sales). Same piracy protections the studio needed.

**The clincher:** The executive's brother had recently taken high school board exams. "By the way, we also safely delivered the examination papers for your brother's boards and carried the return answer sheets."

**Result:** Deal signed within two months.

**Principle demonstrated:** One example that passes the Sinatra Test ("If we can make it there, we can make it anywhere") establishes credibility that no number of ordinary references can match.

### Wendy's "Where's the Beef?" - Testable Credentials

**Setup:** 1984. Wendy's genuinely had bigger burger patties than McDonald's and Burger King.

**The campaign:** Three elderly women staring at an enormous bun. Lift the top: tiny patty, single pickle. Clara Peller: "Where's the beef?"

**Impact:** Customers who believed Wendy's Single was larger than the Whopper or Big Mac increased 47% in two months. Revenue up 31% in the first full year.

**Principle demonstrated:** Testable credentials - the implicit challenge is "go compare for yourself." The audience becomes the authority. No statistics needed, no expert testimony. Just: see for yourself.

### Edible Fabrics - Rohner Textil

**Setup:** Environmentalist Bill McDonough hired to make fabric manufacturing toxic-free. Of 8,000 chemicals tested, 7,962 failed. Remaining 38 were "safe enough to eat."

**The proof:** Swiss government inspectors tested water flowing out of the factory. Their instruments showed nothing. They thought equipment was broken. Tested the water going IN (Swiss drinking water). Equipment was fine. The fabric manufacturing process was FILTERING the water cleaner.

**Business results:** Manufacturing costs dropped 20%. Scraps went from hazardous waste (shipped to Spain) to crop insulation (sold to Swiss farmers).

**Principle demonstrated:** Multiple credibility layers - vivid detail (38 out of 8,000), statistics as relationship (cost down 20%), and the Sinatra Test (if the water comes out cleaner than it goes in, the environmental claims must be true).

## EMOTIONAL

### The Rokia Study - Carnegie Mellon

**Setup:** Tested charitable giving with three versions of a Save the Children letter.

**Results:**
| Version | Average Donation |
|---------|-----------------|
| Statistics (millions suffering) | $1.14 |
| Individual (Rokia, 7, from Mali) | $2.38 |
| Both combined | $1.43 |

**The devastating finding:** Adding statistics to the emotional story REDUCED giving. And priming people to think analytically (simple math problem beforehand) reduced giving to Rokia by 46%.

**Principle demonstrated:** The analytical frame of mind suppresses emotional response. If you want action, lead with an individual, not statistics. Never carelessly mix the two.

### "Don't Mess with Texas" - Identity Model

**Setup:** Texas had a massive highway littering problem. Litterers: 18-35-year-old men, truck drivers, who didn't respond to guilt, environmental appeals, or fines.

**The campaign:** Used Texas icons (Dallas Cowboys players). Framed littering as an insult to Texas identity, not an environmental issue.

**The identity model in action:**
- Who am I? A Texan.
- What kind of situation is this? Someone's trashing my state.
- What would a Texan do? Not litter.

**Results:** Litter on Texas highways reduced 72% in six years.

**Principle demonstrated:** Identity appeals (James March's model) beat consequence appeals. People act from "who I am," not "what benefits me."

### "Truth" Campaign vs. "Think. Don't Smoke."

**Philip Morris's campaign:** "Think. Don't Smoke." Treated teens as rational decision-makers. Completely ineffective - may have increased smoking.

**American Legacy Foundation's campaign:** "Truth" - framed smoking as rebellion AGAINST the tobacco industry. Body bags outside Philip Morris HQ. Confrontational, angry, anti-corporate.

**Why it worked:** Teens' identity = rebellious, anti-authority. "Truth" made NOT smoking the rebellious act. It appealed to who teens wanted to BE, not to health statistics they already knew.

**Principle demonstrated:** Understanding the audience's identity is more powerful than understanding the facts. Teens already knew smoking was dangerous. The problem wasn't knowledge - it was identity.

### Floyd Lee - The Pegasus Mess Hall

**Setup:** Army mess hall manager at a base in Iraq. Standard military cafeteria.

**What he did:** Transformed it into "The Pegasus" - tablecloths, mood lighting, quality food preparation, a name and identity.

**Why:** Not about food. About making soldiers feel human, valued, cared for. Operating at Maslow's highest levels (transcendence) in a war zone.

**Principle demonstrated:** "Living in Maslow's Basement" - the assumption that others are motivated by base needs - is usually wrong. Lee wasn't motivated by his paycheck. Soldiers weren't just hungry. Everyone operates at higher levels than we assume.

## STORIES

### Jared Fogle - Subway

**The story:** College student weighing 425 pounds created his own "Subway diet" - 6-inch turkey sub for lunch, foot-long veggie for dinner, plus walking. Lost 245 pounds in a year.

**The corporate response:** Subway's ad agency initially REJECTED the story. Didn't fit the "7 under 6" campaign. No control group. Worried about liability.

**What happened:** A creative director championed it. First commercial aired. Sales increased 18% in the first year.

**Why it's sticky (full SUCCESs):** Simple (one man, one diet). Unexpected (lost weight eating fast food?!). Concrete (425 pounds, 6-inch turkey, foot-long veggie). Credible (real person, verifiable). Emotional (human struggle, transformation). Story (complete narrative arc).

**Key lesson:** The story was SPOTTED, not created. The agency almost killed it because they were thinking analytically. Sticky stories are found in the wild, not manufactured in conference rooms.

### Stephen Denning - World Bank Springboard Story

**Setup:** Mid-level manager wanted the World Bank to embrace knowledge management. Spent months with data, analysis, arguments. Failed completely.

**The story:** A health worker in Zambia, in a small remote town, logged onto the CDC website and found the answer to a malaria treatment question.

**Why it worked:** The story was a springboard - it didn't explain knowledge management. It demonstrated the POSSIBILITY. The audience could project: "If that's possible in a tiny village in Zambia, imagine what WE could do."

**Result:** That single story launched the World Bank's knowledge management transformation.

**Principle demonstrated:** Springboard stories inspire by showing possibility, not by explaining mechanics. They leave room for the audience to fill in their own version.

### Xerox Repair Technicians - Lunchroom Stories

**Setup:** Julian Orr (anthropologist) studied how Xerox repair technicians actually learned their craft.

**Finding:** Technicians learned more from lunchroom storytelling than from official manuals. Stories had a structure: problem → diagnosis → solution → lesson.

**Example:** EO53 error code. Manual says replace part X. Tech did. Still EO53. Replaced Y. Still EO53. Noticed an unusual detail, tried a creative fix - success.

**Impact:** Xerox created a formal story database (Eureka) to capture and share repair stories across the organization.

**Principle demonstrated:** Stories are flight simulators. They prepare people to solve problems they haven't encountered yet by mentally simulating the diagnosis process.

### The Speakers vs. Stickers Experiment (Stanford)

**Setup:** MBA students gave one-minute persuasive speeches. Average speaker used 2.5 statistics. Only 1 in 10 told a story.

**Results:**
- 63% of listeners remembered stories
- 5% remembered any individual statistic
- Stories were 12x more memorable than statistics
- Students rated "polished delivery" as most important, but delivery had near-zero correlation with actual memorability

**Principle demonstrated:** What you say matters vastly more than how you say it. And stories are what people remember.

### NICU Nurse - Pattern Recognition

**The story:** A neonatal intensive care unit nurse noticed a baby's color was off. All monitors showed normal readings. She insisted something was wrong. She was right - the baby had pneumopericardium (air trapped around the heart).

**Why it matters:** The story circulates among NICU nurses as a teaching tool. It's a simulation: if you see a baby that looks wrong despite good numbers, trust your instincts and ACT. Doctors would have missed it because they trusted instruments over observation.

**Principle demonstrated:** Stories transfer expert intuition. The nurse's decades of pattern recognition, compressed into a single narrative, can teach other nurses to trust visual cues over monitors.
